Under The Hood

You may be wondering what is happening under the hood of this little recommendation system. It makes use of various machine learning and data evaluation techniques as well as several APIs. Let me tell you more about it...

As a first step, I downloaded more than 100.000 New York Times (NYT) articles via their Article Search API. However, I only got the articles' headlines, keywords, lead paragraphs and some short text snippets. The articles belonged to 14 different categories, like "Politics", "Technology", "Style", etc. Then this text data was cleaned, combined and vectorized with a term frequency-inverse document frequency vectorizer. Next, I trained a Logistic Regression model, using several one vs. all classifiers, to predict the articles' sections based on their text features. However, in a similar way as the words in a NYT article indicate the section it belongs to, words in tweets are likely to point to the users interests, segmented in the same categories. Therefore, the obtained model is used to predict a user's interests.

When a Twitter handle is provided above the system connects to the Twitter API and downloads the user's latest 100 tweets. They are processed in the same way as the articles above and the Logistic Regression model is used to predict the category that the user is most likely to be interested in.

Once this section is known, the engine connects to the NYT Top Stories API, which provides the 30 most popular articles from a given section, which is chosen as the previously predicted one. This, however, still leaves us with 30 different articles. In the last step, the recommender calculates the Jaccard distance between all words in the users tweets and the words in the article data. Finally, the closest article is recommended.

As for many recommendation problems, the model works well when there is a lot of data, in this case tweets, available and when this data is not noisy, which means that most of the tweets would belong to the same category. This is of course not always the case and rather an exception, but often nevertheless a reasonable recommendation can be made. Finally, there are many ways to further improve the recommendation engine. On the one hand, the model itself could be improved and one could try to train stronger classifiers. On the other hand, one could recommend not only one but several articles from different probable categories. Or even further newspapers other than the New York Times could be included, both for model training as well as recommendation.
